01 — The problem

Auscultation is the cheapest cardiac diagnostic we have. It's also one of the least reliable.

Cardiovascular disease accounts for roughly a third of global deaths. The stethoscope is everywhere — but expert cardiologists hit ~80% diagnostic accuracy from heart sounds, while general practitioners often fall below 40%.

That gap is a tooling problem, not a knowledge problem. This work closes it with a portable embedded device: a Bluetooth digital stethoscope feeding phonocardiogram (PCG) audio into a low-power FPGA that performs MFCC feature extraction and CNN inference end-to-end, on-device, in real time.

The model was trained on the PhysioNet 2016 Challenge dataset (3,000+ labelled PCG recordings) and deployed as an 8-bit quantized CNN onto Microchip's PolarFire SoC Icicle Kit — a hybrid 5-core RISC-V + FPGA-fabric platform.

02 — System architecture

One chip. Two pipelines.

The PolarFire SoC partitions cleanly: the RISC-V cores handle SPI I/O, DMA, and orchestration; the FPGA fabric runs the latency-critical pipelines — feature extraction (top row, teal) and CNN inference (bottom row, amber) — in parallel, with 8-bit fixed-point arithmetic.

Figure · Two-row serpentine pipeline inside the PolarFire SoC. Top: MFCC feature extraction. Bottom: CNN inference. Strip: RISC-V control.
03 — Methodology

Four stages, one device.

Raw audio enters from a 3M Littmann CORE Bluetooth stethoscope, bridged through an ESP32 over SPI. From there, everything happens on the PolarFire — no PC, no cloud, no round-trip.

01 / acquire

PCG ingestion

Bluetooth audio at 1–2 kHz sampling, ESP32 → SPI bridge into the FPGA.

02 / condition

Filter & frame

High-pass filter strips DC, Hanning windows over 25 ms frames with 10 ms hop, energy-thresholded S1/S2 segmentation.

03 / extract

MFCC features

STFT → triangular Mel bank → log → DCT, yielding 13-dimensional cepstral vectors per frame. Implemented in fixed-point HDL.

04 / classify

Quantized CNN

2 conv + 2 FC layers, 8-bit weights, deployed via Libero SoC. Pipelined MAC units with dynamic frequency scaling (200–667 MHz).

04 — Results

Faster than CPU. Cooler than GPU. Comparable accuracy.

Evaluated on the PhysioNet 2016 dataset, the FPGA implementation achieves a 2-3× latency reduction over published CPU and GPU baselines while drawing under 2 W — well inside the thermal envelope for a portable, battery-powered diagnostic device.
